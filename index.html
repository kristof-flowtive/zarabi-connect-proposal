The Situation

What's Likely Slowing You Down

From the brief, I can read between the lines on where curated B2B prototypes usually stall when they try to grow up. Here is what I would expect you are running into.

The prototype proves the idea, not the experience

It looks the part in demos but does not yet feel like a product a manufacturer would trust their brand to.

Onboarding is the entire product

In a profile-driven network, weak onboarding means thin profiles, and thin profiles kill matching quality from day one.

Buyers and manufacturers are not the same user

One generic signup flow forces both sides through the wrong questions, which is why most B2B directories feel hollow.

Matching is the hardest invisible feature

Recommendations need clear inputs, weights, and a way to explain themselves, or they feel random and lose trust quickly.

Trust signals are doing a lot of quiet work

In a curated network, verified badges, fair attendance, and references are not decoration. They are the reason a buyer replies.

You need a developer who thinks in product, not tickets

The brief makes that explicit. The work needs judgement on UX behaviour, edge cases, and trade-offs, not pixel-pushing.

The Fix

Proposed Solution

I will rebuild the prototype as a polished Next.js MVP with three first-class onboarding flows. Buyer, brand, and manufacturer, each tailored to what that side of the market actually needs to disclose to be matched well. Every flow is multi-step with progress tracking, save and continue later, and a structured profile output that becomes the input to the matching engine.

On top of that sits a clean discovery experience. Search and filtering for active browsing, a recommendation feed for passive discovery, and a trust layer of verification indicators that quietly signal which counterparties have been validated. The whole thing is built mobile-first with the kind of restrained, premium aesthetic the jewellery space expects.
